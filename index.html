Abstract

Abstract: This report details the development and implementation of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and non-neural network models (NNNs) to classify clothing articles using the famous FashionMNIST dataset and determine the optimal approach for classifying this dataset. The experiment explored Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), Support Vector Machines (SVM) , Random Forest(RF), K-Nearest Neighbors(kNN), Gradient Boosting Machines(GBM) and two Convolution Neural Network(CNN) machine learning modes to classify the images into distinct clothing categories. The two CNN models, 4Convolutional Layers, 4Fully Connected Layers (4XConv- layers 4XfC-layers(CNN-1) and 5Convolutional Layers, 5Fully Connected Layers (5XConv- layers 5XfC-layers(CNN-2) were evaluated to improve the classification. CNN-2 was selected as the best model for FashionMNIST dataset classification since it outperformed all the other models in all areas of metrics evaluation, showing an overall accuracy of 91.5% on the testing dataset. Recall, F1-score, and computational efficiency were metrics utilized to evaluate the performance to select this optimal algorithm. Even though its architecture may offer a better and a more detailed feature extraction, it also risked overfitting due to its complexity as compared to CNN-1, which was observed during model evaluations. Additionally, although CNN2 seemed to have a more complex architecture with additional layers, the performance improvement from CNN-1, which had an overall accuracy of around 90.9% on the test, to CNN-2 wasn't substantial. GBM models also demonstrated competitive performance, achieving accuracy levels around 85% to 89% on the FashionMNIST dataset. While not as high performing as CNNs, other NNNs showed good precision and recall across different classes. Introduction

FashionMNIST, a dataset comprising 70,000 grayscale images across 10 classes, presents a comprehensive challenge for machine learning models due to its complex nature and the resemblance between different categories. The study emphasizes the importance of clothing item classification in computer vision and its practical applications, such as in inventory management.

Data Preparation, Transformation, and EDA

The FashionMNIST dataset underwent several preparation and transformation steps to make it suitable for the classification models. This process involved normalization, scaling, and augmentation techniques to enhance the dataset's diversity and representativeness. Additionally, exploratory data analysis was conducted to gain insights into the dataset's characteristics, such as Principal Component Analysis

Methods/Experiments

The methodology includes model training, hyperparameter tuning, and performance evaluation. Models were assessed using accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score. Two CNN architectures, CNN-1 (4 convolutional layers, 4 fully connected layers) and CNN-2 (5 convolutional layers, 5 fully connected layers), were the focus due to their advanced feature extraction capabilities. The training process was conducted using the PyTorch library, with emphasis on data transformation, dataset splitting, and efficient data loading techniques.

Conclusion

The study concluded that CNNs outperform NNNs in classifying the FashionMNIST dataset. While both CNN models achieved high accuracy, CNN-2 showed a marginal improvement over CNN-1, indicating that a more complex architecture might lead to better feature extraction but also poses the risk of overfitting.
